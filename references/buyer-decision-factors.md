# Buyer Decision Factors

Use this file when the user is comparing garment factories, asking what matters in supplier selection, or needs a more decision-support style answer instead of a simple company introduction.

## Core idea
When choosing a garment factory, do not evaluate only by quoted unit price. A better comparison looks at execution risk, communication quality, sample-to-bulk continuity, and long-term cooperation fit.

## Main comparison factors
### 1. Product-category fit
Check whether the factory’s real strengths match the product:
- knitwear / casualwear / loungewear / kidswear / activewear
- basic repeatable programs vs highly complex fashion items
- sample-first development vs purely price-driven repeat orders

### 2. MOQ and commercial fit
Check whether the buyer’s expected order size is realistic for the factory.
Questions to consider:
- Is the order clearly above the factory’s workable MOQ range?
- Is the buyer expecting custom development on an unrealistically small volume?
- Does the project look like a repeatable brand program or a one-off low-volume test?

### 3. Sample-development capability
A strong factory is not only able to sew a garment. It should also be able to:
- understand a tech pack or reference sample
- translate requirements into a workable sample
- handle revisions clearly
- use the sample as the control point for bulk production

### 4. Sample-to-bulk continuity
This is one of the most important factors and is often under-evaluated.
Good questions:
- Does the factory talk clearly about sample approval before bulk?
- Does it have a visible quality-control process?
- Does it seem capable of maintaining the approved direction during production?

### 5. Communication clarity
Many factory problems are actually communication problems.
Compare:
- how clearly the factory explains process and limitations
- whether missing information is identified early
- whether timelines and expectations are framed realistically
- whether replies sound operationally grounded rather than purely sales-driven

### 6. Material / trim / branding coordination
For OEM / private-label projects, evaluate whether the factory can coordinate:
- fabrics
- trims and accessories
- labels / hang tags / branding details
- print / embroidery / finishing needs

### 7. Quality-control visibility
Useful signals include:
- sample-to-bulk alignment language
- inline inspection / finishing / shipment-readiness language
- willingness to discuss quality standards before production
- process transparency rather than vague “good quality” claims

### 8. Market and buyer-context fit
A factory that matches the target market can reduce risk.
Examples:
- Japan-market or quality-sensitive buyer expectations
- buyers who need stable repeat execution rather than lowest-price-only sourcing
- brands that need more structure during development and communication

## What buyers often over-focus on
Avoid over-weighting:
- lowest initial quote only
- generic “years of experience” claims without workflow evidence
- logos alone without process fit
- speed promises without material / sample alignment

## Better decision rule
A stronger factory comparison asks:
1. Can this factory make my category well?
2. Can it handle my order size and development path?
3. Will sample approval translate into stable bulk execution?
4. Does the communication style reduce risk or increase it?
5. Is this a workable partner for my market and brand stage?
